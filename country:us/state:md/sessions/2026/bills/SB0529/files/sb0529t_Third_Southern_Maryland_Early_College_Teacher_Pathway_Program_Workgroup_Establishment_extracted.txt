9 Preamble
10 WHEREAS, Maryland faces a critical shortage of qualified teachers, especially in
11 high–need schools and rural areas such as Southern Maryland where local school systems
12 report persistent vacancies in core subject areas and growing challenges in retaining
13 educators; and
14 WHEREAS, The Blueprint for Maryland’s Future calls for expanding high–quality
15 pathways into the teaching profession, increasing college readiness, and improving
16 workforce pipelines in every region of the State; and
17 WHEREAS, Nationally, early college programs have proven to increase college
18 enrollment, degree attainment, and workforce participation — particularly among
19 first–generation and low–income students — and can be leveraged to build stronger and
20 more diverse teacher pipelines; and
21 WHEREAS, Despite strong national evidence of success, Maryland lags behind most
22 states in providing early postsecondary opportunities for its students; and
23 WHEREAS, As of 2023, Maryland ranked low nationally for dual enrollment
24 participation as a percent of high school enrollment at 10% and ranked low nationally for
25 dual enrollment as a percent of undergraduate enrollment at 7%; and
26 WHEREAS, As of 2022, participation in dual enrollment and early college programs
27 in Maryland is overly concentrated at community colleges with only 5% of students enrolled
28 through public four–year institutions — compared to approximately 28%
29 nationally — limiting exposure to bachelor’s degree pathways, including teacher
30 preparation programs; and
31 WHEREAS, Southern Maryland’s geography, limited access to nearby four–year
32 institutions, and workforce needs make it an ideal region for a program that bridges high
33 schools, community colleges, and the University System of Maryland regional higher
34 education centers to create a seamless teacher preparation pathway; and
35 WHEREAS, Establishing a Southern Maryland Early College Teacher Pathway
36 Program will align with the Blueprint’s goals; now, therefore,

14 (a) (1) In this section, “dually enrolled student” means a student who is dually
15 enrolled in:
16 (i) A secondary school in the State; and
17 (ii) An institution of higher education in the State.
18 (2) “Dually enrolled student” includes a student enrolled in a credit or
19 noncredit certificate or license program, course, or sequence of courses that leads to
20 certification or licensure at an institution of higher education in the State.
21 (b) A public institution of higher education may not charge tuition to a dually
22 enrolled student.
23 (c) Subject to subsection (d) of this section, for each dually enrolled student who
24 is enrolled in a public school in the county, the county board shall pay 75% of the cost of
25 tuition for a public institution of higher education in the State.
26 (d) (1) If there is an agreement before July 1, 2020, between a public school
27 and a public institution of higher education in which the public institution of higher
28 education charges less than 75% of the cost of tuition to a dually enrolled student, the
29 county board shall pay the cost of tuition under the existing agreement.
30 (2) Every 2 years, a public school and a public institution of higher
31 education may evaluate and modify an agreement made under paragraph (1) of this
32 subsection.
